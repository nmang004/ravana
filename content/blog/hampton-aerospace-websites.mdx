## Website Essentials for Hampton Aerospace & Tech

Aerospace and tech company websites need specific elements commercial sites don't require.

### Capabilities Showcase

**Technical Competencies**: Clearly articulated technical capabilities in language that demonstrates expertise without disclosing proprietary information.

**Bad Example**: "We provide aerospace engineering solutions."

**Good Example**: "We specialize in computational fluid dynamics analysis for hypersonic vehicle development, with 15+ years supporting NASA Langley atmospheric research programs."

Specificity communicates competence. Vagueness suggests inexperience.

**Certifications & Credentials**: Display prominently:
- ISO 9001, AS9100D, ISO 27001
- CMMC level (when certified)
- Facility security clearance level
- NIST 800-171 compliance
- SBA certifications (8(a), HUBZone, SDVOSB, WOSB)
- Professional certifications (PMP, PE, security clearances held by team)

**Past Performance**: Challenge with government work: most is classified or proprietary. How do you showcase capabilities?

**Solution**: Sanitized case studies describing problem, approach, results without disclosing classified details.

Example: "We developed advanced sensor fusion algorithms enabling real-time data integration from multiple disparate sources, delivering 40% improvement in processing speed while maintaining full security compliance."

**Prime Contractor Relationships**: When allowed, mention supporting relationships: "Subcontractor to Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, BAE Systems" (without disclosing program details).

### Team & Expertise

**Leadership Backgrounds**: Aerospace/tech customers want to know who they're working with.

**Include**: Education, relevant experience, security clearances, industry recognition, publications or patents (when appropriate).

**Technical Staff Depth**: "15 engineers with TS/SCI clearances" or "PhDs in aerospace engineering, materials science, and computational mechanics" without identifying specific cleared individuals publicly.

**Facility Capabilities**: Square footage, specialized equipment (wind tunnels, clean rooms, testing facilities), SCIF availability, proximity to NASA Langley.

### Research & Thought Leadership

Position your team as industry experts through technical content.

**White Papers**: Technical discussions of aerospace challenges, emerging technologies, research findings.

**Conference Presentations**: Papers presented at AIAA, IEEE, or other technical conferences (ensure no classified information).

**Technical Blog**: Analysis of industry trends, technology assessments, methodology discussions.

**Publications**: Link to non-classified published research, patents, or technical articles.

This content serves triple purpose: SEO value, credibility building with technical audiences, and demonstrating deep expertise beyond marketing claims.

### Security & Compliance Infrastructure

Your website itself should reflect security consciousness.

**Hosting**: U.S.-based hosting providers. For contractors handling CUI, consider FedRAMP-authorized hosting (AWS GovCloud, Azure Government).

**SSL/TLS**: Enterprise-grade certificates from recognized CAs. TLS 1.2 minimum, preferably TLS 1.3.

**Form Security**: All contact forms use end-to-end encryption. CAPTCHA prevents automated submissions.

**Access Controls**: Strong password requirements, two-factor authentication for admin access, role-based permissions.

**Compliance Documentation**: Public-facing security policy, privacy policy, incident response process.

### Contact & Business Development

Make it easy for the right people to engage with you.

**BD Team Contact**: Separate business development contact information from general inquiries.

**Secure Communication**: Encrypted contact forms, secure file upload for RFP/RFQ responses.

**Registration Info**: CAGE code, DUNS number, SAM.gov registration status prominently displayed.

**Teaming Opportunities**: If you seek teaming partnerships, make that clear with dedicated page or section.

## Design Considerations for Technical Audiences

Aerospace and tech websites need different design approaches than consumer-focused sites.

### Professional Credibility

**Clean, Modern Design**: Contemporary design communicates you're current with technology. Dated 2010-era design suggests outdated capabilities.

**Technical Sophistication**: Design should feel sophisticated without being flashy. Government customers want competence, not flash.

**Data Visualization**: If showcasing technical achievements, use charts, graphs, and visual data presentation to communicate complex information clearly.

### Content Depth

**Technical Audiences Appreciate Detail**: Unlike consumer sites where simplicity wins, technical decision-makers want comprehensive information.

Your capability statements can (should) include technical depth. Engineering managers researching contractors want real technical detail, not just marketing speak.

**Scannable Structure**: Even technical content needs clear headers, bullet points, and organization enabling quick scanning.

**Downloadable Resources**: Technical data sheets, capability statements, facility information as downloadable PDFs for offline review.

### Performance & Speed

**Fast Loading**: Even with technical content, sites should load quickly. Technical audiences notice slow sites and interpret as general inefficiency.

**Target**: Under 2 seconds on desktop, under 3 seconds on mobile.

## Content Strategy for Government Audiences

What you say and how you say it matters tremendously with government customers.

### Technical Precision

Government audiences expect precision and accuracy.

**Specific Capabilities**: "Experienced in X-band radar signal processing for airborne platforms" not "radar expertise."

**Quantifiable Results**: "Reduced processing latency by 35% while maintaining 99.99% accuracy" not "improved performance."

**Proper Terminology**: Use industry-standard terminology, acronyms, and technical language correctly.

Precision builds confidence. Vagueness suggests inexperience.

### ITAR Compliance

Defense contractors must carefully review all content for ITAR compliance.

**Restricted Content**: Technical data about defense articles, detailed specifications, proprietary manufacturing processes.

**Safe Content**: General capabilities, sanitized past performance, team credentials, facility information.

**Review Process**: ITAR compliance should review all public-facing content before publishing.

One mistake could trigger State Department investigation and contract disqualification.

### Differentiation

Your Hampton competitors likely have aerospace experience too. What makes you different?

**Specific Expertise**: "Only Hampton-area contractor specializing in composite repair for rotorcraft applications."

**Unique Capabilities**: "In-house autoclaves capable of curing components up to 20 feet, largest on Peninsula."

**Track Record**: "100% on-time delivery record across 47 NASA Langley task orders since 2015."

**Location Advantage**: "5 miles from NASA Langley main gate—enabling rapid response and close collaboration."

Specific differentiation wins contracts. Generic capabilities blend into noise.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do we need CMMC certification for our public website?**

Your public website doesn't process CUI so CMMC certification isn't required for the site itself. However, the site should reflect security best practices aligning with CMMC principles. Procurement professionals do review your site as part of capability assessment.

**Can we mention specific NASA programs on our site?**

Only if information is publicly available and unclassified. Use NASA's public program information. Never disclose classified program details. When in doubt, consult your security officer.

**Should we display our security clearance level?**

Facility clearance level (FCL) is appropriate to display. Individual clearances should be described generally ("team holds TS/SCI clearances") without identifying specific cleared individuals publicly.

**How do we showcase past performance without violating NDAs?**

Use sanitized language: "Supported tactical aerospace systems integration" instead of specific program names. Focus on capabilities demonstrated rather than contract details. Describe problems solved and approaches used in general terms.

**Is a commercial website adequate for aerospace contracting?**

Depends on your contracts and capabilities. Simple commercial sites work for basic subcontracting. Sophisticated programs requiring high clearances and technical depth need professional aerospace-focused websites reflecting that capability level.

**What security measures should our website have?**

Minimum: SSL/TLS encryption, secure hosting, encrypted contact forms, regular security updates, backup systems. For contractors handling sensitive work: FedRAMP hosting, advanced access controls, security monitoring, compliance-aligned infrastructure.

**How do we compete with larger aerospace contractors for visibility?**

Hyperlocal targeting ("Hampton aerospace contractors," "NASA Langley area specialists"), technical specialization, and thought leadership content. Compete on specific expertise, not broad generic terms where giants dominate.
